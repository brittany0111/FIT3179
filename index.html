Malaysia's Forestry Scene

Percentage of Forest Reserves Across Malaysian States in 2021

By 2021, Malaysia's forest reserves were heavily concentrated in Sabah, Perak, and Pahang, In contrast, urban centers like Kuala Lumpur and coastal regions such as Penang show little forest cover. This contrast highlights not only where nature thrives, but also where urgent conservation are needed to protect these critical ecosystems.

Forest Reserve Area by State (2003-2021)

Which states are protecting the highest percentage of their land?

Sabah and Perak stand out as strong guardians of their forests, consistently dedicating large portions of their land to conservation. Meanwhile, other states show shifting trends. For example, Kedah improved their protection levels in 2013, whereas Pahang lost ground as development expands in 2009. This changing landscape tells a story not just of policy and land use, but of how each state weighs the balance between progress and preservation.

A Closer Look at Malaysia's Shrinking Forests

Sabah, Sarawak, and Pahang stand out with the highest tree cover loss in Malaysia, not because they neglect their forests, but because they have the most to lose.

These states are home to some of the country's richest and most expansive forests, so even small clearings for logging or agriculture add up to large losses on the map. Their vast green landscapes, once endless, now tell a story of both abundance and vulnerability.

The Forces Behind the Falling Trees

Permanent Agriculture has been the leading force behind forest loss, as vast tracts of land are cleared to make way for oil palm and rubber plantations that fuel Malaysia's economy.

Logging, while often regulated, continues to chip away at old-growth forests, fragmenting habitats and weakening the natural balance that sustains biodiversity.

Signs of Renewal: Tree Cover Expansion (2000-2020)

Similar to the bar chart on tree cover loss, Sarawak, Sabah, and Pahang continue to stand out in tree cover gain. These states record the highest forest regeneration, reflecting their vast land areas and ongoing reforestation or plantation activities. The pattern suggests that regions experiencing the most deforestation are also those with the most efforts and capacity for regrowth.

Overall, Malaysia's forest trends reveal a complex balance between loss and recovery. While extensive deforestation persists in several regions, strong replanting and natural regeneration efforts help offset some of the damage. Sustaining this balance will depend on long-term conservation strategies that prioritize both economic needs and environmental resilience.
